# 5-minute Loom talk track

## 0:00-0:20 - Opening
Hi, my name is Oksana, and today I will briefly present my solution for the TaskFlow case.

I will explain the main reason behind TaskFlow's decline, the market and competitor context, and the strategy I would recommend for the next 12 months.

## 0:20-1:00 - Core problem
My main conclusion is that TaskFlow's problem is not a shrinking market.
The main issue is weakening product-market fit in its core audience and best monetizing markets.

Downloads, MAU, and MRR all declined during 2025, while CPI increased.
At the same time, retention deteriorated and paid churn increased.

## 1:00-1:35 - Why the product is weakening
The strongest signal is retention decline.
D30 retention fell from 12% to 5%, and D90 retention fell from 6.5% to 2.3%.

At the same time, the product has not had a major feature update in eight months, ratings fell from 4.6 to 4.2, and user complaints focus on limited collaboration, no offline mode, and repetitive UI.

## 1:35-2:00 - Why acquisition is not the answer
Acquisition also became less efficient.
CPI increased from $1.8 to $2.6, 62% of installs come from Meta, and organic share is declining.

Approximate paid CAC is now around $26.5, while approximate paid LTV is only about $26.7, so scaling paid acquisition right now would be risky.

## 2:00-2:35 - Market and competitors
The category is still attractive, so I would not recommend leaving productivity.
However, generic to-do apps are under pressure, and competitors like Todoist, TickTick, Any.do, and Microsoft To Do are better positioned.

They ship more often, offer deeper collaboration, stronger planning workflows, and better distribution through SEO, integrations, and ecosystem reach.

## 2:35-3:30 - Recommendation
My recommendation is a turnaround strategy, not a full pivot.
TaskFlow should reposition itself as a calendar-first planner for young professionals, freelancers, and duos.

The strategy has five parts:
1. Improve the core workflow with offline mode, onboarding, UI refresh, recurring task improvements, and stronger calendar parity.
2. Expand collaboration from 3 to 5 users with comments and attachments.
3. Keep pricing broadly stable, but relax free limits and test better packaging.
4. Reduce inefficient paid UA and shift more effort toward ASO, templates, referrals, and creator partnerships.
5. Focus first on high-value English-speaking markets.

## 3:30-4:10 - Roadmap and KPIs
The roadmap starts with stabilization, then differentiation, then selective AI features, and only after that scaling the winning channels.

My target metrics are D30 retention at 8% to 9%, paid churn at 11% to 12%, organic share of installs at 25%, and MRR recovery to around $78k to $85k over 12 months.

## 4:10-4:35 - Risks
The main risks are team bandwidth, competitive pressure, over-investment in AI, runway pressure, and geographic distraction.

That is why I would prioritize a focused turnaround instead of a broader and riskier pivot.

## 4:35-4:50 - Closing
So overall, my conclusion is simple: TaskFlow should stay in the productivity category, avoid a risky full pivot for now, and focus on restoring product value, sharper positioning, and more efficient growth.

Thank you.
